Threat Taxonomy

5 Types of
Phishing Attacks

Not all phishing looks the same. Attackers choose their method based on their target and goal.

01
📧
Most Common
Email Phishing
Mass-scale attacks sent to thousands of recipients simultaneously. Attackers cast a wide net using generic lures like fake bank alerts, package delivery notices, or tax refunds. Even a 1% click rate on 100,000 emails is 1,000 compromised machines.
Real-World Example"Your Netflix account has been suspended. Click here to update your payment information within 48 hours." Sent to 500,000 addresses. The link leads to a pixel-perfect clone of Netflix's login page.
02
🎯
Highly Targeted
Spear Phishing
Precision attacks that use personal information about the victim their name, employer, colleagues, recent purchases — to craft a convincing, personalized message. Success rates can exceed 50% against unprepared targets.
Real-World Example"Hi Sarah, it's Mike from accounting. I've attached the Q3 expense report you asked for last Tuesday." Sarah never asked for a report. The attachment deploys a backdoor.
03
🐋
Executive Targeting
Whaling
Spear phishing aimed at high-value targets: CEOs, CFOs, board members, and IT administrators. A compromised CEO account enables Business Email Compromise (BEC) fraud worth millions.
Real-World ExampleA CFO received an email appearing to come from the CEO while traveling: "Wire $2.3M to this vendor account for a confidential acquisition." $2.3M was wired. The CEO's account had been spoofed.
04
📱
SMS-Based Attack
Smishing
Phishing delivered via SMS text message. People are more trusting of texts than emails, and mobile screens hide URLs, making malicious links harder to inspect. Attackers impersonate banks, delivery services, or government agencies.
Real-World Example"USPS: Your package couldn't be delivered. Schedule redelivery: usps-redeliver.net/confirm" — The site asks for your name, address, and credit card to pay a fake $3 redelivery fee.
05
☎️
Voice-Based Attack
Vishing
Phishing conducted over phone calls. With AI voice cloning now freely available, attackers can impersonate a family member, colleague, or CEO's voice in real time using audio deepfakes.
Real-World ExampleAn employee received a call from someone who sounded exactly like their CTO asking for MFA bypass codes for "urgent server maintenance." The voice was cloned from public recordings.
Red Team Playbook

The Anatomy of
a Phishing Attack

Here's exactly how a cyber criminal executes a phishing attack — step by step, the way a Red Team operator would map it out.

01
Reconnaissance
Target Selection & Research
The attacker identifies their target — an individual, department, or entire organization. They scrape LinkedIn, company websites, and social media to learn employee names, email formats, organizational structure, and current projects. This information makes the eventual phishing email feel legitimate and personalized.
🔍 Red Team Tactic: Attackers use tools like theHarvester, Maltego, and simple Google dorking to build a target profile. The more data they collect, the more convincing the lure.
02
Infrastructure Setup
Building the Deception
The attacker registers a look-alike domain (e.g., payro1l-dept.com instead of payroll-dept.com), clones a legitimate website, and configures an email server to spoof trusted addresses. They may also set up SSL certificates on fake sites so the browser shows a padlock — tricking users who think "HTTPS = safe."
⚠ Common Mistake: HTTPS only means the connection is encrypted — not that the site is legitimate. Attackers exploit this misunderstanding constantly.
03
Weaponization
Crafting the Phishing Email
The attacker writes a convincing email using the target's real name, their boss's name, or a current event the company is dealing with. They embed malicious links disguised with URL shorteners, attach weaponized documents, and engineer the message to create urgency "your account will be suspended," "verify immediately."
🎭 Social Engineering: Fear, urgency, authority, and curiosity are the four emotional triggers attackers exploit. A message from "IT Security" demanding you reset your password hits multiple triggers at once.
04
Delivery
Sending the Lure
The email is sent to hundreds or thousands of targets simultaneously (spray-and-pray) or to a carefully chosen individual (spear phishing). Attackers send emails during business hours — Tuesday through Thursday, 9–11am — when people are busy and more likely to click without thinking.
📊 Statistics: The average phishing email takes only 82 seconds to claim its first victim after being sent to an organization.
05
Exploitation
The Victim Takes the Bait
The victim clicks the malicious link, lands on the fake login page, and enters their credentials. The phishing site captures everything in real time and may even redirect the victim to the real website — so they believe the login worked. If an attachment was opened, malware silently installs itself.
🪤 The Redirect Trick: After stealing credentials, many phishing sites say "incorrect password, try again" then redirect to the real site. The victim never knows anything happened.
06
Post-Exploitation
The Attacker Moves Laterally
With stolen credentials, the attacker logs into corporate systems, email accounts, VPNs, and cloud services. They harvest more credentials, escalate privileges, exfiltrate sensitive data, deploy ransomware, or sell access on dark web markets. The average breach takes 194 days to identify.
🔵 Blue Team Response: Organizations must monitor for anomalous login locations, unusual access times, and lateral movement across the network. SIEM systems correlate these signals.

Incident Response Playbook
01
Identify & Triage
Alert fires in SIEM on suspicious sender domain. Analyst reviews email headers, body, and link destinations. Assigns severity level (P1–P4) and opens incident ticket.
02
Contain the Threat
Block sender domain and IP at the email gateway. Pull the email from all affected inboxes using admin purge tools (e.g., M365 Compliance Center). Prevent further delivery.
03
Assess Blast Radius
Query mail logs to identify every recipient. Check click-tracking data — did anyone open the link? Correlate with EDR logs for signs of credential entry or file downloads.
04
Remediate Compromised Accounts
Force password resets for any user who clicked. Revoke active sessions and OAuth tokens. Enable MFA if not already active. Review sign-in logs for unauthorized access.
05
Update Defenses & Document
Push IOCs to threat intel platforms (VirusTotal, MISP). Update SIEM detection rules. File incident report. Schedule targeted user training for those who clicked.

Detection
Splunk / Microsoft Sentinel
SIEM platforms that aggregate logs from email, endpoints, and network devices. Analysts write detection rules to fire on suspicious patterns like failed SPF, anomalous logins, or known-bad domains.
Endpoint
CrowdStrike / SentinelOne
EDR tools running on every endpoint. They detect malware behavior in real time — even zero-day payloads. Analysts use them to hunt for lateral movement and quarantine infected machines remotely.
Threat Intel
VirusTotal / MISP
Analysts submit suspicious URLs, file hashes, and domains for reputation analysis. MISP enables sharing of IOCs across organizations so one company's detection becomes everyone's protection.
Email Security
Proofpoint / Mimecast
Secure Email Gateways that scan every inbound message for malicious payloads, lookalike domains, and suspicious links. They enforce SPF, DKIM, and DMARC and sandbox attachments before delivery.
Forensics
Wireshark / Volatility
After a confirmed compromise, analysts use packet capture tools to reconstruct what data left the network and memory forensics tools to extract malware artifacts from RAM for post-incident analysis.
Simulation
KnowBe4 / GoPhish
Blue teams use phishing simulation platforms to run controlled campaigns against their own users. Click rates and report rates become measurable KPIs for the security awareness training program.

Psychology of Attack

Why Phishing
Actually Works

Phishing isn't a software bug — it's a human one. Attackers spend years studying how people read email, what makes them click, and how to bypass both spam filters and human skepticism.

⚡

Urgency & Fear

"Your account closes in 2 hours." Artificial pressure short-circuits critical thinking. When people feel rushed or scared, they act without verifying. This is the single most reliable trigger in social engineering.

🎭

Identity Spoofing

Display names are trivially easy to fake. Attackers clone logos, templates, and tone. The sender field you see is almost never verified by email clients — only the actual address is meaningful.

🎯

Asymmetric Cost

Sending a million emails costs almost nothing. A 0.1% success rate yields thousands of compromised accounts. The economics will never stop working in the attacker's favor — which is why training is so critical.

🔗

Domain Tricks

paypaI.com (capital I) vs paypal.com. "rn" looks like "m" in many fonts. Subdomains like secure.paypal.com.attacker.ru. These tricks survive a casual glance every single time.

🧠

Spear Phishing

Your real manager's name. Your job title. A project you're actually working on. Scraped from LinkedIn and company pages in minutes. Personalization dramatically increases believability and click rates.

📧

Email Was Never Secure

SMTP has no built-in authentication. SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are add-ons — and many organizations don't fully implement them. Attackers know exactly which gaps to exploit, and freely available tools automate the process.

Phishing Recognition
Crash Course

Core concepts. Learn the attacker's playbook so you can recognize it in the wild and know exactly what to do.

⚠️

Red Flags to Spot

  • Sender domain doesn't match the claimed organization
  • Urgency language: "act now," "expires today," "suspended"
  • Generic greetings — "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Unexpected attachments, especially .zip, .exe, .docm
  • Requests for credentials, PINs, or codes via email
  • Hover destination doesn't match the visible link text
  • Reply-To differs from the From address
🎯

Types of Phishing

  • Mass phishing — blasted to millions, low personalization
  • Spear phishing — targeted at a specific individual
  • Whaling — targeting executives and high-value roles
  • BEC — CEO/CFO impersonation for wire transfers
  • Clone phishing — replays a real past email with a malicious link
  • Smishing / Vishing — SMS and phone-based variants
🛡️

How to Protect Yourself

  • Pause before clicking any link in an unexpected email
  • Hover over links — verify the real destination URL
  • Verify through a separate channel — phone, known email
  • Enable MFA on every account, especially email and banking
  • Never enter credentials on a page you reached via email link
  • Use a password manager — it won't autofill on fake domains
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT or security team
🚨

If You Already Clicked

  • Close the tab immediately — don't enter anything
  • If you entered credentials, change the password right now
  • Enable MFA immediately if it wasn't already active
  • Report to your IT/security team — don't stay quiet
  • Review recent sign-in activity for suspicious access
  • Run a malware scan if you downloaded any files
  • Check if the same password was reused on other accounts

Key Takeaways
🔍

Display names lie. Always read the actual sending address, not the name shown.

🔗

Hover before you click. The visible link text tells you nothing. The destination URL tells you everything.

🧠

Context is bait. Emails that reference real people, real events, or your company name are more dangerous, not less.

📞

Verify out-of-band. Call the person. Look up the number yourself. Never use a number from the email in question.

🔐

Password managers save lives. They won't autofill on a fake domain. They catch what your eyes miss.
